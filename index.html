The PORTILAME ANCHOR Framework — From real organizational pain to universal leadership design

The Story Line

This framework is grounded in a real company — PORTILAME, a Portuguese wood construction company with 20+ years of history. What follows is not theory. It is lived experience, diagnosed problems, and the courage to restructure everything while the company was still growing at 40% per year.

In the company I have some assets — equipment and machines — that cost a lot of money, from 500 euros to 2 million euros for a single machine. Some of them always depend on a specialist, extremely hard to recruit or train, which scared me and made me feel insecure. But the truth is, I became much less worried when I realized that my most expensive asset is people — who, by the same measure, cost around 20 million euros — and they are also the hardest "machine" to fine-tune. The user manual I have and use today, tomorrow is already obsolete.

— Founder & Partner, PORTILAME

The DNA of PORTILAME

PORTILAME was founded in 2004 by two partners with deep alignment: shared values, complementary skills, and a relationship built on trust before the first business decision was made. This founding DNA — ethical governance, human centrality, quality obsession, and long-term thinking — became the invisible architecture that sustained the company through two decades of growth.

Who likes, comes. Who loves, stays.

Company Values (Operating System, Not Decoration)
Five values lived in decisions, not written on walls: Convergence (alignment), Know-how (mastery), Progress (forward movement), Empathy (human connection), Sustainability (long-term thinking).

Being PORTILAME is carrying a spirit that cannot be learned from a manual, bought at a fair, or imitated with empty speeches. It is living with that improbable mix of naive courage, humor in the hardest moments, and an almost stubborn determination that turns obstacles into chapters of a story worth telling and retelling with pride.

The Timeline — Growth and the Fractures It Created

2004

Two founders. Five employees. Artisanal workshop. A shared dream built on trust, ethics, and wood.

2004-2019

40% annual growth. Expansion to 100+ employees, 400+ projects across 18 countries. Revenue trajectory toward 25M euros.

2019-2020

The inflection point. Rapid growth started fracturing the DNA that had built the company. What got us here would not get us there. But the founders did not yet realize it.

2020

Internal diagnosis: 6 core organizational problems formally identified. The honest confrontation with reality began.

2022-2024

The awakening. The founders finally realized — two years after the diagnosis — that they no longer had the availability, capacity, or knowledge to manage a company that had grown this fast. The hardest act: lowering pride, elevating humility.

2025-2026

Founders step back from operations. Professional C-level and middle management hired for Human Skills. Governance restructured. Three mission-critical departments created: Quality (ISO 9001, ISO 14001, FSC certifications achieved in 2025), People & Culture (harmonious processes, listening, involving, supporting people), and Brand & Communication (adequate, attentive internal communication). 8.1M euro modular factory investment. Digital transformation. Running against time to recover the DNA.

The 6 Diagnosed Problems (2020)

These are not theoretical. They were formally identified through internal assessment. Every growing organization faces variations of these same fractures.

1. Loss of Invisible Control
Before: decisions were fast, information flowed directly, problems were felt early. After growth: information distorts across hierarchy, problems arrive late or filtered, decisions become slower or political. The risk: believing you have control when you no longer do.
2. Ambiguity of Responsibilities
Org chart boxes existed but functions were unclear. Functions existed but authority was not assumed. Authority existed but there was no accountability. Result: grey zones, "not my problem" culture, decisions stuck in limbo.
3. Leadership Bottleneck
The company grows but the leader becomes the unintentional single point of failure. Every critical decision routes back to the same person. Classic pattern: the organization scales but the founder becomes the bottleneck.
4. Silent Cultural Desalignment
Culture was transmitted by proximity, not by system. New hierarchical layers could not learn culture by osmosis. The same message no longer meant the same thing to everyone. Culture stopped being lived and started being interpreted.
5. Operational Complexity Explosion
More projects, more teams, more interfaces. Small errors multiplied exponentially. The system stopped being robust and became fragile. Communication failures became critical.
6. Knowledge Trapped in People
Too much knowledge in heads, not in systems. Processes not formalized. Dependency on "heroes." Organizational intelligence was individual, not institutional. Scale was limited and risk was high.

The Founders' Humility

Only when we — the founders and leaders — became aware that we no longer had the availability, capacity, and knowledge to manage a company that had grown so rapidly and continues to grow, when we lowered our pride and elevated our humility, did we gain the clarity to structure and hire intermediate leaders with great Human Skills, so that we could rise to the challenge. In truth, this rapid growth had been shaking PORTILAME's DNA for seven years, but we only realized it two years ago. Now we are racing against time.

— Founder & Partner, PORTILAME

The Dream We Truly Believe In

We want every proud face of every one of our people to tell us that their children say: “My hero is my father! Children who see their parents come home fulfilled, proud, and inspired. Every employee as a leader from their position. An organic framework so light that people want to carry it, like identity, not obligation.

I Am PORTILAME. We Are PORTILAME. Being PORTILAME is acting with integrity, ethics, and empathy. Building with people, for people, leaving a legacy that endures through time.

— KickOff 2026

Organizational Radar

Seven dimensions measured continuously. Six internal, one external. The Radar is not primarily a self-assessment tool — it is built on indirect behavioural signals captured from daily digital systems: punctuality patterns, non-conformity records, absenteeism trends, communication frequency, project completion rates, and other operational data that reveal organizational health without asking anyone to fill a survey. These behavioural patterns form the foundation. They are then enriched with 360-degree direct evaluation (peers, leaders, cross-functional) and only lastly complemented by self-assessment. The composite score is segmented by dimension, team, and trend — enabling predictive alerts that anticipate risks before they become problems. Prevention, not cure.

7th Dimension: HORIZON — External Benchmarking & Future Calibration
The six internal dimensions measure who we are. HORIZON measures where we stand relative to the world — and where the world is going. It connects every internal people factor evaluated in this framework with external reality: how other organizations assess and develop their people, what criteria they use, how employees' expectations of well-being and quality of life are evolving, and what pain points future talent wants to see resolved. HORIZON provides the signal of what "others expect" and what "our future employees will demand."

KPI Scoring Architecture

Three integrated scoring layers that ensure the ANCHOR Framework remains calibrated, competitive, and visionary over time.

LAYER 1

Radar Score (Internal)

Composite of the 7 dimensions. Built primarily on indirect behavioural data from digital systems (punctuality, non-conformities, absenteeism, communication patterns), enriched with 360° evaluation and self-assessment. Segmented and predictive — anticipating care needs before they become crises. Quarterly cadence. The foundation for all decisions.

LAYER 2

Universal KPI

Benchmarking score that cross-references internal Radar data with external intelligence: industry standards, competitor practices, employee expectation surveys, well-being trend reports, and talent market signals. Ensures the organization at minimum covers what the best in the market are doing. A score below parity triggers recalibration of internal targets.

LAYER 3

Galactic KPI (Visionary)

Predictive, forward-looking target built on cross-referenced and transversal data: workforce demographic shifts, generational expectations, emerging well-being science, regulatory trends, and AI-augmented pattern recognition. The Galactic KPI anticipates what the Universal KPI will become in 2-3 years. It sets the aspirational meta-target that keeps the organization at the vanguard — not reacting to change, but arriving before it.

How the Three Layers Work Together
Radar Score answers: "How healthy are we right now?"
Universal KPI answers: "Are we at least as good as the best out there?"
Galactic KPI answers: "Are we ready for what the world will expect next?"

The gap between Radar and Universal triggers corrective action. The gap between Universal and Galactic triggers innovation and anticipation. Together, they create a dynamic calibration system that ensures every dimension of the ANCHOR Framework has objectives adapted to future realities while always staying ahead.

PORTILAME Well-Being Ecosystem — The Living Proof

The ANCHOR framework is not theoretical. PORTILAME already implements a comprehensive well-being ecosystem that maps directly to the framework’s dimensions. These practices demonstrate that holistic leadership is operational, measurable, and impactful.

Physical Vitality Programme
External community support: Direct and indirect financial funding for sport across all age groups — athletics, football, padel, trail running, and more.
Internal employee support: Financial backing for employees who compete regularly (cycling, padel, futsal, etc.), full branded equipment with company identity (“EU SOU PORTILAME”), and complete backoffice promotion of achievements, values, and efforts.
Facility partnerships: Weekly reserved slots at nearby padel complex and gym, plus discounts for personal enrolment — lowering every barrier to regular physical activity.
ANCHOR mapping: R (Resilience) — biological infrastructure for sustained performance. H (Humanity) — belonging made visible through shared identity.
Knowledge & Identity Rituals
Call Semanal: Every Wednesday after lunch, 30 min (25’ + 5’ Q&A), online, voluntary. Any employee presents their perspective — vision, strategy, difficulties, processes, responsibilities. Prepared with digital and oral content. Recorded and stored for future reference.
Annual Kick-Off: Full-company event where founders, C-level, and leaders present the year ahead — linked to previous results but rooted in DNA stories connecting colleagues across all levels. Emotional, purposeful, unifying.
ANCHOR mapping: N (Narrative) — every voice has a stage. A (Awareness) — cross-functional understanding. C (Cadence) — weekly and annual rhythms.
Structural Equity & Feedback Culture
Clear role definitions, well-structured salary levels, transparency and equity in duties and benefits. Constructive feedback culture. Teams led by leaders who act as moderators and orientators. These structural elements make well-being sustainable rather than performative.
Community & Academic Engagement
Support for local NGOs, sponsorship of intervention artists, continuous relationship with schools, professional schools, and universities — sharing experiences, materials, and knowledge; offering students the opportunity to experience PORTILAME and potentially join. Creates desire, cultivates the brand, evidences pride, validates the DNA.

Diagnostic Rules (Universal)

Before implementing the ANCHOR framework, any organization must run a diagnostic:

1. DNA Clarity — Are the founding values still alive and actionable?
2. Growth vs. Structure Gap — Has the organization outgrown its governance model?
3. Leadership Bottleneck Test — How many critical decisions require the founder/CEO?
4. Cultural Transmission — Can a new employee learn the culture without founders?
5. Knowledge Institutionalization — Is intelligence in systems or in heads?
6. People Investment — Is human development proportional to equipment investment?

Group 3 Findings — When Was Leadership at Its Best?

Proactive, supportive leaders who absorbed employee emotions and provided structure were most effective. Leaders as moderators, not directors. Acknowledgement over instruction. 2. Flexibility and autonomy empower people — especially during disruption.
3. Leaders must communicate openly and share optimism — acknowledging effort, sharing responsibility, accepting imperfection.
4. Proactive support structures matter more than reactive ones — built in, not deployed in crisis mode.
5. Clarity, purpose, and structure reduce uncertainty — leadership that absorbs ambiguity and converts it into clarity.

TUESDAY, MARCH 24

The Power of Presence

Attention, digital habits, focus, psychological safety, and trust

Sessions

Stephanie Reina — The Science of Attention
Attention defined as "taking possession by the mind, in clear and vivid form, of one out of what may seem several simultaneously possible objects or trains of thought" (William James, 1890). Three attentional networks: Alerting, Orienting, Executive Function (Posner & Petersen, 1990). Cognitive resources are limited — inattentional blindness (Mack & Rock, 1998). The Photo Taking Effect: photographing reduces memory of experiences (Henkel, 2014). Cognitive residue: task switching = less productivity + more mistakes (Leroy, 2009; Loukopoulos et al., 2001). Technoference: digital interference harms both couple and parent-child relationships (McDaniel & Radesky, 2018). Short-form video, shorter shots in TV/ads, and double screening are shrinking attention spans (Gloria Mark). Replenishment through nature, physical activity, and mindfulness. Key insight: attention is trainable.
Stephanie Reina — Digital Habit Hacking
Practical session on opportunity cost of app/platform time. Strategies: turn off notifications, remove apps, phone pouch/airplane mode, skip morning/night check, grayscale mode, Freedom/Opal/Rescue Time/Forest/FocusFlight apps, Focusmate, News Feed Eradicator/BeTimeful, Unhook extension, email office hours + schedule send, mindfulness for internal triggers. Two philosophies: Digital Minimalism and "Appstinence". Right to disconnect discussion: unspoken digital norms in teams. Group reflection: where is fragmented attention costing you the most — performance, relationships, or personal life?
Laura Maguire, Ph.D. — Psychological Safety for Trust
Psychological safety = "the belief that you will not be punished or humiliated for speaking up with ideas, questions, concerns, or mistakes." The world needs a different type of leader. Three application dimensions: Making mistakes (How do you react when a team member makes a mistake? Do you share your own?), Acknowledging (Do you acknowledge good work? When were you last acknowledged?), Sense of belonging (When did you last feel a clear sign of belonging to your organization?). Silent resistance is not defiance — it is a safety response. When change threatens autonomy or competence, people comply externally while resisting internally.
Shane O'Connor — Mindfulness
Daily attention-training continuation. Mindfulness as "a state of focused attention in the present moment without judgment." Action step: pick one moment in your day and do it with full, undivided attention — no phone, no multitasking. Just notice.

Day 2 Group Work Prompts — CONDITIONS FOR PERFORMANCE

Individual reflection: In your organization, what helps or hinders focus, trust, and psychological safety?

Group synthesis: What conditions consistently support — or undermine — attention and safety across contexts? Where are the biggest leverage points for change?

Deliverable: 2-3 systemic conditions that strongly influence performance and trust.

Group 3 Findings — Psychological Safety Mapping
What helps: Free psychological support, "good" governance (e.g. no-meeting Fridays), optional mindfulness sessions, transparency about failures.

What hinders: No role models, "wrong" governance, workload and pressure, not listening, missing understanding and awareness, missing trust, missing communication, not enough resources.

Group conclusion: Teamwork, Trust, People over Processes.

Key observation: Low hierarchy with limited governance makes it harder to establish these conditions consistently — reinforcing the need for intentional structure (not bureaucracy) to sustain psychological safety at scale.

Cognitive residue from task switching directly undermines decision quality. At PORTILAME, the founders' recognition that operational overload was destroying strategic capacity is this principle in action.
H — Humanity: Psychological safety as operational "people-first." Maguire's three dimensions (mistakes, acknowledging, belonging) map directly to PORTILAME's journey: the 2020 diagnosis revealed culture being interpreted rather than lived. The new ComEx model (48h counter-proposal) is a structural attempt to rebuild voice. The "Being PORTILAME" manifesto and KickOff sense-of-belonging stories are the emotional infrastructure of safety.
C — Cadence: Digital Habit Hacking is about rhythm discipline. Email office hours, morning/night check elimination, scheduled send — these are cadence choices. At PORTILAME, the weekly ComEx meetings and structured reporting cadence serve the same function: protecting attention and creating predictable space for focus.

Sessions

Maria Jose Ferrari, Ph.D. — Critical Thinking in the Age of AI
Critical thinking as top WEF skill (2025). In the AI era, critical thinking is both shield (against misinformation and bias) and sword (for innovation). Art analysis of "Las Meninas" by Velázquez as exercise in power, performance, and critical interpretation. "Garbage in, garbage out" — the importance of quality input for AI systems. Maria Jose Ferrari, Ph.D. — Metacognition and Decision-Making
Metacognition = "thinking about thinking." Kahneman's System 1 (fast, intuitive) vs. System 2 (slow, deliberate). Systematic errors and cognitive biases affect even trained professionals. Mapping cognitive profiles: strengths (analytical, creative, decisive, open-minded) vs. weaknesses (impulsivity, analysis paralysis, overconfidence, confirmation bias). Self-assessment + peer reflection to uncover blind spots. "Not because we think something is correct, it necessarily is — our brains make mistakes."
Victor Gay Zaragoza — The Leader's Narrative: Legitimacy, Trust & Meaning
Storytelling biologically boosts oxytocin (trust, happiness) and reduces cortisol (stress) — measurable in clinical settings. Narrative Economics: one-third rule for company value — revenue, brand (story), and future value. Tesla vs. Toyota: narrative drives stock beyond revenue. The Triple Challenge for leaders: distrust, fear, digital overload. Four Principles of Effective Storytelling: (1) Sell the dream, not the product, (2) Empathy Generation Strategy — emotional connection from the start, (3) Anchor in authentic emotion and real experience, (4) Adjust for modern overwhelmed audiences. Julius Caesar's "Commentaries on the Gallic Wars" as the ultimate leadership narrative.
Shane O'Connor — Mindfulness: Purpose, Movement & Self-Compassion
80% of communication flows body-to-mind, not the reverse. Identifying where purpose resides in the body. Self-compassion: "Are you compassionate to yourself?" Using the same kindness toward yourself as toward a loved one after a mistake. Guided meditation on breath, body awareness, and nurturing achievement memory.

That metacognitive leap took two years. Cognitive biases (overconfidence, confirmation bias) explain why growing organizations resist restructuring even when evidence is clear.
N — Narrative: Gay Zaragoza's four storytelling principles are the science behind what PORTILAME did instinctively at KickOff 2026: (1) sold the dream ("children saying my hero is my father, I want to work at PORTILAME"), (2) opened with empathy (around 100 families, immense responsibility), (3) anchored in authentic emotion ("Being PORTILAME" manifesto), (4) adapted for the audience (Menti poll, visual storytelling). R — Resilience: Self-compassion (O'Connor) is the missing link in high-performance leadership. At PORTILAME, the founders' acknowledgement of vulnerability ("lowered pride, elevated humility") is exactly this — applying the same kindness to yourself that you would give a loved one. Sustainable leadership requires recovery of the self, not just the system.

Key Insights from Daniel de la Serna

The Brain’s Energy Economy
The brain uses three fuel sources: glucose (primary), ketones (during fasting), and lactate (produced by muscles during physical activity — not waste, but brain fuel). Metabolic flexibility — the ability to switch between substrates — is a critical health marker. When stress demands more brain energy, blood flow redirects from other organs. With only 5 litres of blood, this is a zero-sum game: brain performance comes at the cost of other systems.
Decision-Making Is Expensive
Humans solve stress through decision-making — not eating, not running. But decision-making requires enormous metabolic resources. The more substrates available (metabolic flexibility), the more capacity for good decisions. Leaders who are metabolically depleted make worse decisions. This has direct implications for meeting schedules, break rhythms, and decision fatigue in organizations.
Brain vs. Immune System: The Energy Trade-Off
The brain and immune system are the two most energy-expensive organs — and they cannot both operate at peak simultaneously. Chronic inflammation (from lifestyle, stress, or poor nutrition) forces the immune system to compete with the brain for resources. Result: fatigue, reduced cognitive performance, and impaired leadership capacity. Protecting against chronic inflammation is protecting leadership capacity.
Physical Activity: The Universal Intervention
“The most powerful intervention to protect the brain is to be more physically active.” Exercise produces lactate (brain fuel) AND increases mitochondrial density (energy production capacity). Mitochondria need acute stress to maintain themselves — comfort destroys them. Cold exposure, intermittent fasting, and physical effort all promote mitochondrial health. Modern comfort is biologically new; our systems evolved for periodic discomfort.
The Comfort Paradox
“Too much comfort has an impact on our health because our biology evolved to handle acute stressors, not chronic ease.” Organizations that eliminate all friction, discomfort, and challenge from work are inadvertently degrading the metabolic resilience of their people. The goal is not discomfort for its own sake — it is purposeful challenge within a supportive structure. This is the biological foundation of ANCHOR’s Resilience dimension.

Circadian Rhythms & Energy Management

Daniel de la Serna — The science of timing, light, and biological synchronization

Biorhythms: The Foundation of Human Performance
Almost all bodily processes are temporally coordinated through circadian rhythms — the 24-hour biological clock. Each organ has specific periods of heightened activity and rest, synchronized primarily by sunlight (the principal zeitgeber), food intake timing, and temperature changes. Central clocks (brain/hypothalamus) and peripheral clocks (organs) must stay synchronized. Desynchronization — from jet lag, shift work, or irregular schedules — is the first step toward chronic disease, even before symptoms appear. The 2017 Nobel Prize in Medicine recognized this circadian research.
Cortisol & Melatonin: The Dual Rhythm
Cortisol peaks in the early morning, energizing the body for performance. Melatonin rises at night, regulating sleep and cellular repair (including mitochondrial repair). Chronic stress disrupts cortisol patterns; artificial blue light suppresses melatonin by up to 70% with just one hour of evening exposure. When we lose rhythm in cortisol or melatonin production, we suffer the consequences — this pre-symptomatic phase of disease appears long before clinical diagnosis.
Practical Chronobiology for Leaders
Maximize morning natural sunlight exposure (anchors cortisol cycle). Schedule high-decision meetings during cortisol-aligned hours. Filter or eliminate blue light 1–2h before sleep. Keep consistent meal times within the first 12 hours post-waking. Respect chronotypes (early birds vs. night owls) — sensitivity to light varies but natural exposure can reset patterns. Three days in nature can fully reset a disrupted body clock. Organizations that respect these rhythms protect their leaders’ decision-making capacity.

Mindfulness Day 4: Discovering Blind Spots

Shane O’Connor — What you’re not seeing might be right in front of you

The Blind Spot Exercise
“Blind spots are things we’re looking at but can’t see.” Using the classic letter-F counting exercise, O’Connor demonstrated that even with clear instructions, people consistently miss what’s in front of them — the brain has ~240,000 scotomas (blind spots). Applied to health: what are you overlooking in your nutrition, breathing, sleep, or stress management? The inefficient breather averages 26,000 breaths/day when 10,000–15,000 is optimal. Lasting change comes from authentic choice (“I choose, I can”), not obligation.
Multidimensional Nutrition
Well-being nutrition has four dimensions, not one: (1) Edible nutrition — what you eat; (2) Social nutrition — connections and interactions; (3) Biophilic nutrition — time in nature; (4) Passion/outlet nutrition — hobbies and creative engagement. Also introduced empathic joy as the antidote to envy — gaining energy from others’ happiness rather than feeling diminished. This concept directly connects to ANCHOR’s Humanity (Belonging) dimension.

PORTILAME in Practice: Well-Being as Organizational DNA

How PORTILAME already lives these principles — evidence that the ANCHOR framework is not theoretical but operational

External Sports Support
PORTILAME actively supports community sport across all age groups through direct and indirect financial funding: athletics, football, padel, trail running, and more. This positions the company as a community anchor while reinforcing its identity as a well-being-oriented organization. It builds brand visibility, local trust, and employee pride in belonging.
Internal Employee Sports Programme
Employees who regularly compete in sports (cycling, padel, futsal, and others — no sport excluded) receive direct financial support, full branded equipment with company identity (“EU SOU PORTILAME” — the literal embodiment of wearing the jersey), and complete backoffice support for promotion of their achievements, values, and efforts. This celebrates individual identity within collective belonging, and creates indirect inspiration: colleagues who do not normally practice sport see the visibility and culture of physical vitality and are drawn toward it.
Sports Facility Partnerships
PORTILAME maintains direct partnerships with nearby sports facilities (padel complex, gym) providing weekly time slots for dynamic, regular use among employees, plus additional discounts to encourage personal enrolment and the development of healthy personal routines. This removes friction: the proximity and financial accessibility lower every barrier to regular physical activity — exactly what De la Serna prescribes for mitochondrial health and metabolic flexibility.
Call Semanal — Weekly Knowledge Sharing
Every Wednesday after lunch, 30 minutes (25’ presentation + 5’ Q&A), online, voluntary. Any employee presents their perspective: vision, strategy, difficulties, processes, responsibilities — all previously prepared with digital and oral content. It gives stage to every person, sensitizes the entire organization to each role’s reality, and builds lateral understanding. Sessions are recorded and stored in a repository for future reference. This is Narrative (N) and Awareness (A) in practice: every voice matters, every perspective counts.
Annual Kick-Off
At the start of each year, founders/partners, C-level, and team leaders present to the entire company: where the organization is heading, how it is structuring itself, where it wants to position. Linked to the previous year’s results at all levels, but deeply rooted in the organizational DNA since 2024 — recalling stories connecting colleagues across every level, sharing professional and personal narratives that occurred within the workplace and among colleagues. This event involves people, reconnects them to the emotional bonds that unite everyone and to the company’s purpose: we are all working toward the same mission.
Structural Foundations of Well-Being
Beyond sports and events, PORTILAME embeds well-being into its organizational structure: clearly defined roles and responsibilities, well-structured salary levels with transparency and equity in duties and benefits, a culture of constructive feedback, and teams led by leaders who act as moderators and orientators rather than commanders. These structural elements — often invisible but deeply felt — are what makes well-being sustainable rather than performative.
Community, Academic & Social Impact
PORTILAME supports local NGOs, promotes and sponsors intervention artists, and maintains a continuous relationship with the academic world (schools, professional schools, universities) — sharing experiences, materials, and support; exchanging knowledge; and offering students the opportunity to experience PORTILAME’s culture and potentially join the team. This creates desire, cultivates the brand, evidences the pride of those already there, validates the organizational DNA and its foundation in the most noble human values.

Key Insights from Mario Sebastián — Cisco Case Study

A New Way of Leading: Strengths-Based Organization & Appreciative Inquiry at Enterprise Scale

The Problem: When Fixing Makes Things Worse
Cisco CX EMEA faced declining business, reorganizations, lay-offs, fear, and disengagement. Mario’s instinct was to fix the problem: more control, more meetings, focus on issues. The result? Things got worse. The natural “fixing reflex” — the default response of most leaders under pressure — was itself the problem. More structure, more reporting, more oversight did not rebuild trust or energy. It amplified fear.
The Turning Point: One Question Changed Everything
“Tell me about a time things worked really well.” When Mario asked this single Appreciative Inquiry question, the energy in the room shifted immediately. People stopped defending and started remembering. Three discoveries emerged: focus shapes behaviour (what you pay attention to grows), energy drives performance (not compliance), and fixing ≠ improving (solving problems returns you to baseline; building on strengths takes you beyond it).
The Journey: From Challenge to Strengths-Based Organization
Cisco’s transformation followed a clear arc: Challenge → Opportunity → Strategy (3-year plan + Vision) → Implementation → Results. The implementation centred on building a Strengths-Based Organization using CliftonStrengths, the Team Space tool, strengths workshops, individual coaching, weekly check-ins focused on what people do best, and quarterly engagement surveys. The leader’s role shifted from “decision maker” to “context creator” — someone who creates conditions for others to excel and grow.
Appreciative Inquiry at Cisco: The 4D Cycle in Practice
Mario applied the same AI cycle we use in ANCHOR: Discovery (what works when we are at our best?), Dream (what if every team operated from strengths?), Design (strengths-based tools, coaching, check-ins), Destiny (embed into culture, measure through engagement). His core philosophy: “An organization is not a problem to be solved but a solution to be embraced.” This is Appreciative Inquiry at its purest — and it worked at the scale of a $1.2B global operation.

Act 5a: Design — The ANCHOR Framework (60 seconds) — Slide 6

The ANCHOR Framework
We built a framework from real organizational pain. Six dimensions, three games, one anchor:

AWARENESS (Being) — Self and system. Know yourself (Character Strengths), know your biases (System 1 vs. 2), know your attention limits (44 seconds average).
NARRATIVE (Trust) — Purpose and meaning. Storytelling changes brain chemistry (oxytocin up, cortisol down). "Being PORTILAME" is not a slogan — it is the narrative that held the culture together.
CADENCE (Health) — Rhythms and rituals. Digital habit discipline. Weekly ComEx. Monthly recognition. Quarterly radar. Annual recalibration.
HUMANITY (Belonging) — People-first. Psychological safety (mistakes, acknowledgement, belonging). Three departments created for the mission: Quality, People & Culture, Brand & Communication.
ORGANIZATION (Growth) — Adaptive structure. Governance that enables, not constrains. Roles clear enough that autonomy is safe.
RESILIENCE (Mindfulness) — Sustainable energy. Self-compassion. Circadian alignment. Recovery is not weakness — it is strategy.

Inner Game (A+N): Who you are. Outer Game (C+H+O): How you operate. Long Game (R): How you sustain.

Act 5b: Design — The Calibration Engine (30 seconds) — Slide 7

HORIZON + Three-Layer Radar
Six dimensions measure who we are. HORIZON measures where the world is going.

The Radar is built primarily on indirect behavioural signals from digital systems — punctuality, non-conformities, absenteeism, communication patterns — enriched with 360° direct evaluation and only then self-assessment. This composite score is segmented and predictive: it anticipates risks before they become problems. Prevention, not cure.

Three scoring layers: Radar Score (how healthy are we?), Universal KPI (are we at least as good as the best?), Galactic KPI (are we ready for what comes next?). The framework self-calibrates over time.
